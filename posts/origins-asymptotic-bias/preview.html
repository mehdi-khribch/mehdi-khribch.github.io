Origins of the Asymptotic Bias of SNIS

2026-04-07

Everyone “knows” that the self-normalised importance sampling estimator has bias \(O(1/N)\). It is one of those folk results that gets stated without proof in textbooks, invoked casually in papers, and waved away as obvious. While working on our paper [@djkw], we decided to actually prove it—and in the process, went looking for who first wrote the formula down. This is the story of that search, and of the surprisingly subtle gap between knowing a formula and proving it.

The formula everyone knows

Self-normalised importance sampling (SNIS) is the workhorse of Monte Carlo: given i.i.d. draws \(x_1, \ldots, x_N\) from a proposal \(q\), the estimator \[ \hat{F}_N = \frac{\sum_{n=1}^N \omega(x_n) f(x_n)}{\sum_{n=1}^N \omega(x_n)}, \qquad \omega(x) = \frac{\pi(x)}{q(x)}, \] targets \(\pi(f)\) but is biased, because \(\mathbb{E}[A/B] \neq \mathbb{E}[A]/\mathbb{E}[B]\) in general. The “well-known” leading-order bias is \[ \mathbb{E}[\hat{F}_N] - \pi(f) \approx -\frac{1}{N} \int (f(x) - \pi(f))\, \omega^2(x)\, q(dx). \] You can find this formula (or close variants) in @liu2008 (Section 2.5), in countless lecture notes, and in the kind of back-of-the-envelope delta-method calculations that get passed around at conferences. But where does it actually come from? And has anyone ever proved it?

The search: a PhD thesis from 1988

During the preparation of [@djkw], my co-author Pierre Jacob started digging into the history. The trail led to a surprising place: a PhD thesis from 1988.

Timothy C. Hesterberg, working under Brad Efron at Stanford, wrote a thesis titled “Advances in Importance Sampling” [@hesterberg1988]—Technical Report No. 300, Department of Statistics, Stanford University. On page 40, in Section 2.5.2 (“Bias”), Hesterberg writes down the bias formula as his equation (2.63). In his notation (where \(f\) is the target, \(g\) the proposal, \(W = f/g\) the weight, \(\theta\) the test function, and \(Y = \theta W\)): \[ E[\hat\mu_{\text{ratio}} - \mu] = -\frac{1}{n}\, E_g[W(Y - \mu W)] + \text{lower-order terms}. \] Translating to our notation (\(\pi\) for target, \(q\) for proposal, \(\omega = \pi/q\), \(f\) for test function), this becomes exactly: \[ \mathbb{E}[\hat{F}_N - \pi(f)] = -\frac{1}{N}\, q(\omega^2(f - \pi(f))) + \text{lower-order terms}. \]

As far as we can tell, this is the oldest explicit statement of the SNIS bias formula in the literature. Hesterberg also published a Technometrics paper [@hesterberg1995] covering related material, but the bias formula appears most clearly in the thesis itself.

How Hesterberg derives it (and where he stops)

Hesterberg’s derivation has two layers, neither of which constitutes a proof of the moment result.

Layer 1: Delta method. In Appendix 3 (pp. 215–218), he writes \(\hat{F} = \bar{Y}/\bar{W}\) as a ratio of sample averages and performs a second-order Taylor expansion around \((\mathbb{E}_q[\omega f],\, \mathbb{E}_q[\omega]) = (\pi(f),\, 1)\). Setting \(C = \overline{\omega f} - \pi(f)\) and \(D = \bar\omega - 1\), one expands \((\pi(f) + C)/(1 + D)\) and takes expectations term by term, using \(\mathbb{E}[C] = \mathbb{E}[D] = 0\): \[ \mathbb{E}[\hat{F}] \approx \pi(f) - \mathbb{E}[CD] + \pi(f)\,\mathbb{E}[D^2] = \pi(f) - \frac{1}{N}\, q(\omega^2(f - \pi(f))). \] This is elegant but purely formal: the Taylor expansion is truncated and its expectation taken term by term, with no control on the remainder.

Layer 2: Edgeworth expansion. To lend this analytical support, Hesterberg appeals to the Edgeworth expansion of @bhattacharya1978 (their Theorem 2), which gives the distributional approximation: \[ \mathbb{P}\!\left\{\sqrt{N}(\hat{F} - \pi(f)) \leq z\sigma\right\} = \Phi(z) + \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\!\left(-\frac{b}{\sigma} + \frac{\kappa_3}{6\sigma^3}(z^2 - 1)\right)\phi(z) + o(N^{-1/2}), \] where \(b = -q(\omega^2(f - \pi(f)))\) is the “bias term” read off from the expansion.

And then Hesterberg writes something remarkable on page 40:

“These are the first order bias terms in the Edgeworth expansion of the distributions of these estimates. These are more useful than the actual biases, which may be infinite or undefined.”

He knows that what he has is a distributional result, not a moment result, and he explicitly flags that the actual expectation \(\mathbb{E}[\hat{F}]\) might not even exist. This is intellectually honest and, as it turns out, mathematically sharp.

The gap: CDF \(\neq\) moments

This brings us to what I think is the most interesting part of the story, and the piece that motivated me to understand the distinction more carefully.

The Edgeworth expansion tells you about the distribution of \(\sqrt{N}(\hat{F} - \pi(f))\)—it approximates the CDF. But knowing the CDF to high accuracy does not tell you the mean. This is a basic fact of probability, but it is worth pausing on because it is so easy to forget.

Why not? The mean of a random variable \(X\) can be written as a tail integral: \[ \mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^\infty (1 - F_X(t))\, dt - \int_{-\infty}^0 F_X(t)\, dt. \] If the CDF \(F_X\) is approximated by \(\Phi + r_N\) with \(|r_N(t)| = o(N^{-1/2})\) uniformly in \(t\), you might hope to integrate this to get the mean. But the integral \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty r_N(t)\, dt\) need not be small—or even finite. The CDF error is pointwise small (it vanishes at every fixed \(t\)) but it is not \(L^1\)-small (it might not be integrable). The tails of \(r_N\) can decay as slowly as \(|t|^{-2}\), making the integral diverge even though \(\sup_t |r_N(t)|\) is tiny.

A counterexample. Here is a clean construction that drives the point home. For each \(N \geq 1\), let \(Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\) and let \(V_N\) be independent of \(Z\) with \[ V_N = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{with probability } 1 - 1/N, \\ N^{3/4} & \text{with probability } 1/N. \end{cases} \] Define \(T_N = Z + V_N/\sqrt{N}\). Since \(V_N = 0\) with probability \(1 - 1/N\): \[ F_{T_N}(z) = (1 - 1/N)\,\Phi(z) + (1/N)\,\Phi(z - N^{1/4}) = \Phi(z) + O(N^{-1}) \quad \text{uniformly in } z. \] The CDF looks Gaussian to \(O(N^{-1})\)—the Edgeworth “bias term” is \(b = 0\). But: \[ \mathbb{E}[T_N] = \frac{N^{3/4}}{N \sqrt{N}} = N^{-3/4} \neq 0. \] The perturbation \(V_N\) is a rare, large spike: it happens with probability \(1/N\) (invisible to the CDF at any fixed \(z\)), but when it fires it contributes \(N^{3/4}/\sqrt{N} = N^{1/4}\) to \(T_N\), which is enough to move the mean. The CDF sees nothing; the expectation feels everything.

The missing ingredient: uniform integrability. The classical tool for bridging this gap is uniform integrability. By @billingsley1999 [Theorem 3.5]: if \(X_N \xrightarrow{d} X\) and \(\{X_N\}\) is uniformly integrable, then \(\mathbb{E}[X_N] \to \mathbb{E}[X]\). A sufficient condition is \(\sup_N \mathbb{E}[|X_N|^{1+\delta}] < \infty\) for some \(\delta > 0\). The Edgeworth expansion provides no such control, and Hesterberg does not supply it.

For more on this distinction, see @bhattacharya1986 [Chapter 2] who discuss explicitly when Edgeworth expansions yield valid moment approximations, and @hall1992 [Chapter 2, Section 2.5] who makes the same point in the bootstrap context.

Our result: a rigorous bias theorem

In [@djkw], we prove what appears to be the first rigorous version of the asymptotic bias formula.

Theorem (Theorem 2.1 in @djkw)

Let \(f\) with \(\pi(|f|) < \infty\). Assume \(x_1, \ldots, x_N\) are i.i.d. from \(q\), \(\omega = \pi/q\), and \(\hat{F}_N = \sum \omega_n f_n / \sum \omega_n\). If \(q(|\omega^2(f - \pi(f))|^{1+\epsilon}) < \infty\) for some \(\epsilon > 0\) and \(q(\omega^{-\eta}) < \infty\) for some \(\eta > 0\), then: \[ \lim_{N \to \infty} N \times \mathbb{E}[\hat{F}_N - \pi(f)] = -q(\omega^2(f - \pi(f))). \]

The proof avoids Edgeworth expansions entirely. Instead, it uses the exchangeability of the sample to isolate a single particle \(x_1\) and write the rescaled bias as an expectation \(-\mathbb{E}[A_N]\) where \(A_N = \omega_1^2(f_1 - \pi(f)) / (\hat{Z}_N \cdot \hat{Z}_{N-1}')\) (with \(\hat{Z}'_{N-1}\) the leave-one-out average). By the strong law, \(A_N \to \omega_1^2(f_1 - \pi(f))\) a.s. Then the key step: we establish uniform integrability of \(\{A_N\}\) by bounding \(\sup_N \mathbb{E}[|A_N|^{1+\epsilon}] < \infty\). The numerator is handled by the moment condition on \(\omega^2(f - \pi(f))\); the denominator is controlled by a standalone result (Proposition 2.1 in our paper) that bounds inverse moments of the sample average of \(\omega\) using only \(q(\omega^{-\eta}) < \infty\). Vitali’s convergence theorem then lets us exchange limit and expectation.

Compared to Hesterberg’s conditions (third moments of \(\omega\) and \(\omega f\) under \(q\), plus Cramer’s condition on the characteristic function), our assumptions are strictly weaker: we need only slightly more than second moments, no Cramer condition, and we get a genuine moment result rather than a distributional approximation.

An independent result on the same asymptotic bias was obtained by Kamelia Daudel and co-authors [@daudel2024], whose Proposition 6 applied to IS yields the same leading term under separate moment conditions on \(\omega\) and on \(\omega(f - \pi(f))\). We show in our appendix that the conditions of @daudel2024 imply ours but not conversely, so our theorem is strictly more general—but credit where credit is due, the result was found independently.

The moral

There are a few takeaways from this story that I find interesting beyond the specific result.

First, folk results deserve proofs. The SNIS bias formula had been floating around for nearly 40 years without a rigorous proof. Hesterberg had the formula and the intellectual honesty to flag the gap; everyone after him (including textbook authors) simply repeated the formula without comment. It took actually sitting down to write a proof to discover that the standard approach (Edgeworth expansions) is fundamentally insufficient.

Second, distributional results and moment results are different things, and the gap between them is not a technicality—it is a genuine mathematical obstacle that requires a specific tool (uniform integrability) to bridge. The counterexample above shows this is not pedantry: you can have perfect CDF approximation and completely wrong moments.

Third, searching the literature is underrated. Pierre’s detective work in tracking down the Hesterberg thesis—a scanned Stanford technical report from 1988, not indexed in the usual databases—saved us from either failing to cite the originator or, worse, claiming novelty for the formula itself rather than for its proof. The formula is Hesterberg’s; what is new is that it is actually true (under mild conditions).
